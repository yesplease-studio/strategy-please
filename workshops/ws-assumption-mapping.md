## When to run this

- The company is about to make a significant investment (product build, market entry, hiring, campaign) based on beliefs that haven't been validated
- A strategy or plan feels compelling but is built on assumptions nobody has tested
- Team members use phrases like "we believe," "we think," or "our customers want" without evidence
- The company has failed at something and wants to understand what assumptions were wrong
- A new initiative or pivot needs a risk assessment before committing resources

Works across maturity levels 1-4 and across multiple dimensions. This is a cross-cutting workshop: it doesn't build capability in one dimension but instead surfaces the assumptions underneath any strategic plan. Especially useful early in an engagement (to de-risk before deeper work) or late (to validate before execution).

This workshop works well as a standalone 60-90 min session. It's naturally self-facilitatable because the framework is structured enough to guide the conversation without an outside perspective.

## What participants will answer

1. What are we assuming is true that we haven't validated?
2. Which assumptions would break our strategy if they turned out to be wrong?
3. What's our confidence level for each critical assumption, and what's it based on?
4. How would we test the assumptions that matter most, cheaply and quickly?

## Agenda

| Time | Block | Purpose |
|------|-------|---------|
| 0:00 - 0:10 | Opening | Set context: every strategy is built on assumptions. The goal is to find them and rank them. |
| 0:10 - 0:30 | Assumption surfacing | Brain-dump every assumption underlying the current strategy, plan, or decision |
| 0:30 - 0:50 | Assumption mapping | Place each assumption on the importance/confidence matrix |
| 0:50 - 1:10 | Test design | For the critical assumptions (high importance, low confidence): how would we test each one? |
| 1:10 - 1:25 | Action planning | Commit to testing 2-3 assumptions. Define the test, timeline, and what "validated" means. |
| 1:25 - 1:30 | Close | Summarize the assumption map, the test plan, and review cadence |

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Assumption Surfacing (20 min)

Rapid brainstorm of every assumption the team is operating on. Quantity over quality. No filtering.

**Key prompts:**
- "We assume our customer wants X" (What do we assume about customer behavior, preferences, willingness to pay?)
- "We assume the market will Y" (What do we assume about timing, trends, competitive dynamics?)
- "We assume we can Z" (What do we assume about our own capacity, skills, resources, timeline?)
- "We assume our product does W" (What do we assume about product-market fit, use cases, retention?)
- Look at recent decisions: what was assumed but not tested before the decision was made?

**What it produces:** A raw list of 20-40 assumptions. Most teams are surprised by how many beliefs are driving their strategy without validation.

### Exercise 2: The Assumption Matrix (20 min)

Place each assumption on a 2x2 matrix:
- **X-axis:** Confidence (how sure are we this is true? Low to High)
- **Y-axis:** Importance (how much does our strategy depend on this? Low to High)

**Key prompts:**
- If this assumption is wrong, what breaks?
- What evidence do we have that this is true? (Anecdote? Data? Gut feel?)
- Would a skeptical outsider accept our evidence?
- Can we afford to be wrong about this?

**What it produces:** A visual map with four quadrants:
- **High importance, low confidence:** These are the critical risks. Test these first.
- **High importance, high confidence:** The foundation. Monitor but don't test.
- **Low importance, low confidence:** Interesting but not urgent. Ignore for now.
- **Low importance, high confidence:** Known truths about things that don't matter much. Ignore.

### Exercise 3: Test Design (20 min)

For each assumption in the "high importance, low confidence" quadrant, design a cheap, fast test.

**Key prompts:**
- What's the fastest way to get evidence? (Customer conversation, data analysis, small experiment, desk research?)
- What would convince us this assumption is true? What would convince us it's false?
- How long should the test take? (Target: days to weeks, not months)
- What's the minimum cost and effort?
- Who runs the test?

**What it produces:** A test plan for 2-3 critical assumptions, each with: the test method, success/failure criteria, timeline, owner, and cost.

### Exercise 4: Commit and Schedule (15 min)

Pick the 2-3 tests that are highest priority and commit to running them.

**Key prompts:**
- Which test, if the assumption proves wrong, would change our next decision the most?
- What do we do with the results? (Proceed, pivot, gather more data?)
- When do we reconvene to review results?
- What decision are we explicitly holding until the test results are in?

**What it produces:** A concrete action plan with 2-3 tests committed, owners assigned, and a review date set.

## Expected outputs

1. **Assumption inventory** with 20-40 assumptions surfaced and categorized
2. **Assumption matrix** showing all assumptions mapped by importance and confidence
3. **Critical assumptions** (3-5) identified as high importance, low confidence
4. **Test plans** for the top 2-3 critical assumptions with method, criteria, timeline, and owner
5. **Decision holds** listing which decisions are paused pending test results

## What typically comes next

- Run the tests (days to weeks)
- **Assumption review session** (30-60 min) to evaluate test results and update the map
- If critical assumptions are validated: proceed with the strategy
- If critical assumptions are invalidated: run the relevant workshop (Strategic Clarity, ICP Discovery, Positioning, etc.) to rebuild the foundation
- Update the company profile with validated/invalidated assumptions

## Board layout

**Frame 1: Assumption Brain Dump**
Large open area with stickies. Four prompt columns to seed the brainstorm: "Customer assumptions," "Market assumptions," "Capability assumptions," "Product assumptions."

**Frame 2: The Assumption Matrix**
2x2 grid. X-axis: "Low Confidence" to "High Confidence." Y-axis: "Low Importance" to "High Importance." Quadrant labels: "Test these first" (top-left), "Monitor" (top-right), "Ignore" (bottom-left), "Known truths" (bottom-right). Stickies moved from Frame 1.

**Frame 3: Test Design**
One card per critical assumption. Each card has: assumption text, test method, success criteria, failure criteria, timeline, owner, estimated cost.

**Frame 4: Action Plan**
Priority-ordered test list. For each: what we're testing, who's running it, when results are due, what decision it informs. Review date highlighted.
