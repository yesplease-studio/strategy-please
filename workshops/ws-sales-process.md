## When to run this

- Deals happen through founder relationships and inbound luck, with no repeatable process
- There's no qualification framework: the team invests equal time in every prospect regardless of fit
- Common objections come up repeatedly but there's no documented response strategy
- Deals stall at predictable points (after demo, during budget approval, competitor evaluation) with no system for moving them forward
- The company is about to hire its first sales person and needs a process to hand over, not just a contact list

Most useful at maturity levels 1-3 on Dim 6 (Sales Process and Conversion). Works best when Dim 3 (ICP) is defined (so you know who to qualify) and Dim 2 (Positioning) has some foundation (so you know what to say). If the company doesn't know its customer or can't explain its value, those workshops come first.

## What participants will answer

1. What does our sales process actually look like today, end to end?
2. Where do deals stall, and what happens when they stall?
3. What do we know about prospects who close vs. prospects who don't? Can we tell the difference earlier?
4. What objections come up repeatedly, and how do we handle them?
5. What would a new hire need to know to run this process on their own?

## Agenda

| Time | Block | Purpose |
|------|-------|---------|
| 0:00 - 0:10 | Opening | Set context: a sales process isn't a script. It's a system for consistently turning interest into revenue. |
| 0:10 - 0:30 | Process mapping | Map the current sales process from first touch to close, including what actually happens (not what should happen) |
| 0:30 - 0:50 | Stall point analysis | Identify where deals stall, why, and what's been tried to move them forward |
| 0:50 - 1:10 | Qualification and objection handling | Build a qualification framework and draft responses to the top 3 objections |
| 1:10 - 1:25 | Process design | Define the target sales process with stages, criteria, and actions at each stage |
| 1:25 - 1:30 | Close | Summarize the new process, assign ownership, set the review date |

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Process Map (20 min)

Map what actually happens from the moment a prospect enters the pipeline to the moment a deal closes (or dies). Focus on reality, not aspiration.

**Key prompts:**
- Walk me through the last deal you closed. Every step, from first touch to signed contract.
- Now walk me through the last deal you lost. Where did it diverge from the win?
- How many touches does a typical deal require? How long from first touch to close?
- Who's involved on your side at each stage? Who's involved on the customer's side?
- Where does the process differ depending on who's running it? (This reveals what's personal vs. systemic)

**What it produces:** A visual process map showing the real stages of the sales cycle, typical timelines, and who's involved at each point. The gap between "how it should work" and "how it actually works" is diagnostic.

### Exercise 2: Stall Point Analysis (20 min)

Identify where deals consistently slow down or die.

**Key prompts:**
- Where do most deals stall? After the demo? During budget approval? When they go to evaluate competitors?
- When a deal stalls at [stage], what do you do? What usually happens next?
- How many stalled deals eventually close vs. how many die? Do you know?
- Is there a point in the process where you can predict the outcome? (If you can predict at stage 2 that a deal won't close, you're spending stages 3-5 on a lost cause)
- What's the difference between a deal that stalls and recovers vs. one that stalls and dies?

**What it produces:** A stall point map showing the 2-3 most common stall points, the typical behaviors when deals stall, and the patterns that predict recovery vs. death.

### Exercise 3: Qualification Framework and Objection Handling (20 min)

Build the tools for evaluating prospects early and handling the objections that come up repeatedly.

**Key prompts (qualification):**
- What 3-5 things must be true for a deal to close? (Budget exists, decision-maker is engaged, problem is urgent, timeline is real)
- At what stage should each of these be confirmed?
- What's the earliest point where you can confidently say "this deal will not close"?
- What do you do with a prospect that doesn't qualify? (Disqualify, nurture, or requalify later?)

**Key prompts (objections):**
- What are the top 3 objections you hear repeatedly?
- For each: what's the real concern behind the stated objection?
- What response has worked? What response hasn't?
- Are there objections that are actually disqualification signals? (If "we don't have budget" means "this isn't a priority," that's a different problem than "budget is locked until Q3.")

**What it produces:** A qualification checklist with stage-specific criteria, and documented responses for the top 3 objections with the reasoning behind each response.

### Exercise 4: Target Process Design (15 min)

Design the sales process the team will use going forward.

**Key prompts:**
- Based on today's work: what are the stages? (Keep it to 4-6 stages max)
- What needs to be true to move from one stage to the next? (Entry criteria for each stage)
- What's the action at each stage? (What does the seller do?)
- How do you track it? (CRM stages, spreadsheet, whatever works today)
- If you hired someone tomorrow, could you hand them this document and they'd know what to do?

**What it produces:** A defined sales process with stages, entry criteria, actions, and a system for tracking. Simple enough to follow consistently.

## Expected outputs

1. **Current process map** showing the real sales cycle stages, timelines, and participants
2. **Stall point analysis** identifying the 2-3 most common stall points with patterns and recovery strategies
3. **Qualification framework** with stage-specific criteria for advancing or disqualifying prospects
4. **Objection playbook** with the top 3 objections, the real concern behind each, and documented responses
5. **Target sales process** with defined stages, entry criteria, and actions at each stage

## What typically comes next

- Implement the target process in the CRM (or start tracking in a spreadsheet if no CRM exists)
- Test the qualification framework on the current pipeline: does it correctly predict outcomes?
- 30-day review: is the process being followed? Where does it break?
- **Competitive Landscape Workshop** if objection handling revealed competitive gaps
- **Positioning Workshop** if the sales process revealed that prospects don't understand the value proposition
- **Growth Channel Workshop** if the process is solid but the pipeline is too thin

## Board layout

**Frame 1: Current Process Map**
Horizontal flow: stages from left to right. Each stage is a box with: stage name, typical timeline, who's involved (company side), who's involved (customer side), key action. Arrows between stages. Red markers where deals commonly stall.

**Frame 2: Stall Point Analysis**
One card per stall point. Each card: where it happens, what the prospect typically says/does, what the team typically does, whether the deal usually recovers or dies, and what a better response might be.

**Frame 3: Qualification Framework**
Table format: stages across the top, qualification criteria down the side. Green checkmarks where each criterion should be confirmed. Red flags that trigger disqualification at each stage.

**Frame 4: Objection Playbook**
Three cards, one per top objection. Each card: the stated objection, the real concern behind it, the recommended response, and what to do if the response doesn't land.

**Frame 5: Target Process**
Clean version of the process map (from Frame 1) with the improvements applied. Stages, entry criteria, actions, and tracking method. Highlighted: what changed from the current process.
