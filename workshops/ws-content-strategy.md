## When to run this

- The company has no content, or content is sporadic with no strategy behind what gets published
- Content exists but it's product-focused: feature announcements, company updates, generic blog posts that could be published by any competitor
- There's no editorial voice, no point of view, no reason for the audience to follow along
- The founder knows the company should be publishing but doesn't know what to write about or why it would matter
- Content has been tried (perhaps a freelancer or agency was hired) and the output was generic

Most useful at maturity levels 1-3 on Dim 5 (Content and Thought Leadership). Works best when Dim 2 (Positioning) and Dim 3 (ICP) are at least partially defined. Content without a clear audience and message is noise.

## What participants will answer

1. What does our ICP need to hear from us that they can't get elsewhere?
2. What do we know from experience that our audience is still figuring out?
3. What topics, if we owned them, would make prospects think of us first?
4. What's the simplest publishing cadence we can maintain without it becoming a chore?
5. How does content connect to our sales process? What would a prospect read that makes the sales conversation easier?

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Expertise Audit (20 min)

Surface what the company knows from experience that the audience is still figuring out.

**Key prompts:**
- What questions do customers ask in the first sales conversation that you could answer in public?
- What mistakes do you see companies in your space making repeatedly?
- What do you believe about your industry that most people haven't caught on to yet?
- What advice do you give customers after they start using your product that you wish they'd known before?
- What's a question you're tired of answering individually that you could answer once, publicly?

**What it produces:** A list of 10-15 expertise topics grounded in real experience. These aren't content ideas yet; they're the raw material. The best content comes from practitioners sharing what they've actually observed, not from keyword research.

### Exercise 2: Topic Ownership (20 min)

From the expertise audit, select the 3-5 topics the company will own. "Own" means: when someone in the ICP thinks about this topic, the company should come to mind.

**Key prompts:**
- Which of these topics connects most directly to the problem your ICP has?
- Which topic, if you published about it consistently, would make prospects trust you before the first sales call?
- Which topic can you write about from genuine experience, not from research? (Experience-based content is distinctive. Research-based content is commodity.)
- Which topic has the least competition? Where can you be the definitive voice?
- For each topic: what's the company's specific point of view? (Not "content marketing is important" but "most B2B content fails because it's written for algorithms, not for the person at 8am who needs an answer.")

**What it produces:** 3-5 owned topics, each with a specific point of view. This becomes the editorial backbone: every piece of content maps to one of these topics.

### Exercise 3: Content Engine Design (20 min)

Design the minimum viable system that produces consistent output without becoming a burden.

**Key prompts:**
- What format feels natural? (Short-form LinkedIn posts, long-form articles, video, podcast?) Start with what the team will actually do, not what they think they should do.
- What's the minimum cadence that builds momentum? (Weekly is ideal. Biweekly is fine. Monthly loses the thread.)
- Who writes? (Founder, team member, ghostwritten from conversations?) Be honest about capacity.
- What's the distribution channel? (LinkedIn, newsletter, blog, industry publications?) Pick one primary.
- What does the workflow look like? (Draft → review → publish? Or just publish?)
- How long should creation take per piece? (If it's more than 2 hours per piece at the current cadence, the system will break.)

**What it produces:** A content engine spec: format, cadence, channel, workflow, and owner. Simple enough to actually follow.

### Exercise 4: First Five (15 min)

Plan the first five pieces of content. Making it concrete removes the "what do I write about?" barrier.

**Key prompts:**
- For each owned topic: what's the single best first piece? The one that demonstrates your point of view most clearly?
- What's the hook? (The first sentence that earns the reader's attention)
- What experience or example makes this credible? (Not theory; something you've actually seen.)
- What should the reader do or think differently after reading this?
- Can you write this in under an hour? (If not, it's too ambitious for piece #1.)

**What it produces:** Five content pieces planned with: topic, title/angle, hook, key point, and the experience that makes it credible. Enough to start publishing this week.

## Expected outputs

1. **Expertise inventory** with 10-15 topics grounded in real experience
2. **Owned topics** (3-5) with specific point of view for each
3. **Content engine spec** defining format, cadence, channel, workflow, and owner
4. **First five content plan** with titles, angles, hooks, and the experience behind each
5. **First publish date** committed and calendared

## What typically comes next

- Publish the first piece within one week of the workshop
- Follow the engine spec for 90 days before evaluating
- 90-day review: what worked, what didn't, what to adjust
- **Positioning Workshop** if content creation reveals that the positioning isn't sharp enough to write from
- **Growth Channel Workshop** if content becomes a viable growth channel worth investing in further

## Board layout

**Frame 1: Expertise Audit**
Large open area with stickies. Four prompt columns: "Customer questions," "Industry mistakes," "Contrarian beliefs," "Advice we repeat." Each sticky is one expertise topic.

**Frame 2: Topic Ownership**
Five slots for owned topics. Each slot has: topic name, point of view (one sentence), connection to ICP, competitive density (low/medium/high). Topics selected from Frame 1, highlighted or moved.

**Frame 3: Content Engine**
Six fields: "Format," "Cadence," "Channel," "Who writes," "Workflow," "Time per piece." One sticky per field with the decision.

**Frame 4: First Five**
Five cards in a row. Each card: topic (from owned topics), title/angle, hook (first sentence), key experience, and target publish date.

**Frame 5: Commitments**
First publish date (large, highlighted). Engine owner. 90-day review date. Any supporting decisions (e.g., "Set up LinkedIn newsletter by Friday").
