## When to run this

- Pricing was set once (often based on what competitors charge or what felt reasonable) and hasn't been revisited
- The company doesn't know whether it's leaving money on the table or pricing out its ICP
- There's no clear rationale for the pricing model (why subscription vs. usage-based vs. per-seat)
- Customer acquisition is working but unit economics aren't understood: CAC, LTV, and payback period are unknown or vague
- The company is preparing for a funding round and needs to articulate a defensible revenue model

Most useful at maturity levels 1-3 on Dim 4 (Revenue Model and Pricing). Works best when Dim 3 (ICP) has at least some definition. Pricing decisions depend on who you're selling to and what they value.

## What participants will answer

1. How do we make money, and why this model?
2. What would happen if we doubled our prices tomorrow?
3. What does our best customer pay vs. our average customer? What's the expansion path?
4. Are we pricing for the customer we have or the customer we want?
5. What's the relationship between our price and the value the customer receives?

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Pricing Anatomy (20 min)

Map the full pricing structure as it exists today. Not just the sticker price, but the entire commercial model.

**Key prompts:**
- What do you charge, and how? (Per seat, per usage, flat fee, tiered?)
- Why this model? Was it a deliberate choice or inherited?
- What does your average customer pay? What does your best customer pay? What's the ratio?
- What percentage of revenue comes from your top 10% of customers?
- How does pricing change as customers grow? Is there a natural expansion path?

**What it produces:** A clear map of the current pricing model with its strengths, weaknesses, and the reasoning (or lack thereof) behind it.

### Exercise 2: Value Gap Analysis (20 min)

Assess whether the price reflects the value the customer receives.

**Key prompts:**
- What measurable outcome does your product deliver? (Time saved, revenue increased, cost reduced)
- Can you put a number on that outcome for your typical customer?
- If the customer pays X and receives 10X in value, you have pricing power. If it's 2X, you're vulnerable. Where are you?
- Who are you too expensive for? Who are you too cheap for? Are those the right trade-offs?
- Have you ever lost a deal on price? What was the real reason?

**What it produces:** A value-price ratio assessment that reveals whether the company is underpricing (common in early-stage), overpricing (rare), or misaligned (pricing for the wrong metric).

### Exercise 3: Model Exploration (20 min)

Evaluate 2-3 alternative pricing structures against the company's ICP and growth goals.

**Key prompts:**
- If you switched to [alternative model], what would change for your best customers? For your worst?
- Which model aligns price with the value metric? (If you save customers time, price on time saved. If you process transactions, price on volume.)
- Which model creates a natural expansion path? (Customers pay more as they get more value)
- Which model has the lowest friction for new customers? (Important for growth)
- What model would your ICP expect? What's familiar in your category?

**What it produces:** A comparison of 2-3 pricing models evaluated against value alignment, growth potential, customer expectations, and operational complexity.

### Exercise 4: Pricing Decision (15 min)

Select the pricing direction and define what to test.

**Key prompts:**
- Based on the analysis: keep the current model, adjust it, or switch?
- If adjusting: what specifically changes? New tiers, new metric, new price point?
- What's the smallest test we can run? (Grandfather existing customers, test new pricing on new deals only)
- What would success look like after 90 days of the new pricing?
- What's the risk if we get this wrong? How reversible is it?

**What it produces:** A pricing decision with a test plan: what changes, for whom, how to measure success, and the rollback plan.

## Expected outputs

1. **Pricing model map** documenting the current structure, rationale, and identified weaknesses
2. **Value-price ratio assessment** showing whether the company is capturing appropriate value
3. **Pricing recommendation** with the selected direction (keep, adjust, or switch) and specific changes
4. **Test plan** for validating the pricing change: scope, timeline, success criteria, and rollback plan
5. **Expansion revenue path** documenting how customers naturally upgrade or pay more over time

## What typically comes next

- Execute the pricing test on new customers
- 90-day review to evaluate test results
- **Positioning Workshop** if the pricing change requires updated messaging
- **Growth Channel Workshop** if the pricing change opens new market segments
- Update sales materials and website with the new pricing

## Board layout

**Frame 1: Pricing Anatomy**
Three sections: "Current model" (structure, tiers, metrics), "Customer distribution" (what different segments pay), "The reasoning" (why this model was chosen). Stickies mapping the full commercial structure.

**Frame 2: Value Gap**
Two columns: "Value delivered" (measurable outcomes for the customer) and "Value captured" (what the company charges). Lines connecting them. Gap analysis at the bottom: overpricing, underpricing, or misaligned.

**Frame 3: Model Exploration**
Three columns, one per alternative model. Rows: "How it works," "Impact on best customers," "Impact on worst customers," "Growth potential," "Operational complexity." Traffic light ratings.

**Frame 4: Decision and Test Plan**
Selected pricing direction (large text). Below: what changes, who it applies to, success criteria, timeline, rollback plan. Owner assigned.
