## When to run this

- The homepage doesn't explain what the company does, for whom, or why it matters in the first scroll
- Different team members pitch the product differently in sales conversations
- The company describes itself in internal/product language rather than customer language
- Prospects frequently say "I don't really understand what you do" or ask basic clarifying questions late in the sales process
- The positioning could describe several competitors equally well, with no clear differentiator

Most useful for companies at maturity levels 1-3 on Dim 2 (Positioning and Messaging). Works best when Dim 3 (ICP) is at least partially defined. If the company doesn't know who its customer is, run the ICP Discovery Workshop first.

This workshop requires a half-day because positioning work involves building and testing multiple framings. Rushing it produces generic output.

## What participants will answer

1. What is the one thing we want every prospect to believe after talking to us?
2. What do our happiest customers say about us that we didn't expect?
3. What do we do that no competitor does, or that we do fundamentally differently?
4. If we had to explain what we do on a billboard, what would it say?
5. What's the story? How did this product come to exist, and why now?
6. What do we refuse to do that competitors are willing to do?

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Audit (30 min)

Pull up the company's website, recent pitch deck, and any sales materials. As a group, answer:

**Key prompts:**
- Does the homepage explain what we do, for whom, and why it matters in the first scroll?
- What words appear most often? Are those customer words or internal words?
- If a prospect landed on this page with no context, what would they think we do?
- How is this different from what [competitor] says?

**What it produces:** An honest baseline of the current positioning with specific observations about what works, what's generic, and what's missing. This grounds the rest of the workshop in reality rather than aspiration.

### Exercise 2: Customer Voice Collection (30 min)

Gather the actual language customers use. Pull from support tickets, sales call transcripts, reviews, testimonials, NPS verbatims, or have participants recall direct quotes.

**Key prompts:**
- What do happy customers say when they refer us?
- What surprised us about how customers describe our value?
- What words do customers use that we don't use in our marketing?
- What problem do customers say we solve? Is that the problem we think we solve?

**What it produces:** A bank of customer language organized by theme. The gap between company language and customer language is itself a diagnostic finding.

### Exercise 3: Competitive Differentiation (30 min)

Map the competitive landscape from the customer's perspective.

**Key prompts:**
- When we win deals, what's the reason?
- When we lose deals, what's the reason?
- What do we do that no competitor does?
- What do we refuse to do that competitors are willing to do?
- Is our biggest competitor another product, the status quo, or an internal workaround?

**What it produces:** A differentiation map with honest assessment of strengths, weaknesses, and the specific angles where the company genuinely wins.

### Exercise 4: Positioning Drafts (30 min)

Using inputs from Exercises 1-3, draft 3-5 positioning statements. Each draft follows the format:

**For** [target customer] **who** [has this problem], **[company name]** is the [category/frame] **that** [key differentiator]. **Unlike** [alternatives], **we** [unique advantage].

Encourage variety: one draft might lead with the problem, another with the approach, another with the outcome.

**What it produces:** Multiple positioning candidates that can be tested and refined.

### Exercise 5: Pressure Test (30 min)

Take each positioning draft and test it against real scenarios:

**Key prompts:**
- Read this aloud as if you're on a sales call. Does it sound natural?
- Put this on the homepage. Would a prospect understand what you do?
- Say this to an investor. Does it make them want to know more?
- Tell this to a happy customer. Would they nod and say "yes, that's right"?

**What it produces:** A clear winner (or a strong direction to refine) with notes on what works in which context.

## Expected outputs

1. **Positioning statement** (primary) with clear articulation of: who it's for, what problem it solves, what makes it different, and why now
2. **Supporting proof points** connecting the positioning to real evidence (customer quotes, competitive advantages, product capabilities)
3. **Current-state gap analysis** identifying where existing materials (website, pitch, sales conversations) need to be updated
4. **Customer language bank** of real phrases and descriptions to use in marketing and sales
5. **Competitive talk tracks** for the 2-3 most common competitive scenarios

## What typically comes next

- Update the website homepage and key landing pages to reflect the new positioning
- Revise the sales pitch and demo script
- **Competitive Landscape Workshop** if the competitive differentiation exercise revealed significant gaps in competitive knowledge
- **Growth Channel Workshop** to build distribution strategy around the new positioning
- Brief the team on the positioning so everyone tells the same story

## Board layout

**Frame 1: Current State Audit**
Screenshot or text of current homepage. Three columns below: "What works," "What's generic," "What's missing." Stickies in each column.

**Frame 2: Customer Voice**
Four quadrants: "How they describe us," "Unexpected compliments," "The problem they say we solve," "Words they use that we don't." Stickies with direct quotes.

**Frame 3: Competitive Map**
Center: company name. Surrounding: competitor names. Between each pair, two-way stickies: "Where we win" and "Where they win." Bottom section: "Our real differentiator" and "What we refuse to do."

**Frame 4: Positioning Drafts**
Five numbered boxes, one per draft. Each box has the positioning statement text and space for "Strengths" and "Concerns" stickies.

**Frame 5: Pressure Test Results**
Matrix: drafts across the top, scenarios down the side (Sales call, Homepage, Investor, Customer referral). Traffic light rating (green/yellow/red) in each cell with notes.

**Frame 6: Selected Positioning and Next Steps**
Final positioning statement (large text). Below: proof points, gap analysis items, and assigned next steps with owners and dates.
