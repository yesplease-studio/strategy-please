## When to run this

- The company describes its customer as a category ("B2B SaaS companies") rather than a specific person with a specific problem at a specific moment
- Different team members give different answers about who the best customer is
- Marketing and sales efforts feel scattered because there's no clear targeting criteria
- The company has customers but hasn't analyzed what the best ones have in common
- Win rates are inconsistent and nobody can explain why some deals close easily while others drag

Most useful for companies at maturity levels 1-3 on Dim 3 (ICP Definition and Focus). At level 1, the company hasn't thought about ICP at all. At level 2, there's an intuitive sense but nothing documented. At level 3, the ICP is defined at a company level but not at a persona level with buying triggers and qualifying criteria.

This workshop fits naturally as a standalone 60-90 min session for founders or small teams who need to get one thing right before building further.

## What participants will answer

1. Who are our best customers today, and what specifically makes them "best"?
2. What problem were they experiencing at the moment they decided to look for a solution?
3. What do our best customers have in common that our worst customers don't?
4. Are we selling to the person who feels the pain, the person who holds the budget, or both?
5. If we could clone one customer 100 times, who would it be and why?

## Agenda

| Time | Block | Purpose |
|------|-------|---------|
| 0:00 - 0:10 | Opening | Set context: why ICP matters, what we're building toward, ground rules |
| 0:10 - 0:30 | Customer stories | Each participant tells the story of their best and worst customer. Listen for patterns. |
| 0:30 - 0:50 | Pattern extraction | Identify common traits across best customers: company profile, persona, pain, trigger, buying behavior |
| 0:50 - 1:05 | Segment prioritization | Define 2-3 ICP segments, rank by fit and opportunity, identify primary vs. secondary |
| 1:05 - 1:20 | Qualifying criteria | For the primary segment: what signals tell you this prospect is a good fit? What disqualifies? |
| 1:20 - 1:30 | Close | Summarize outputs, assign ownership for next steps |

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Best/Worst Customer Stories (20 min)

Each participant identifies their single best and single worst customer and tells both stories in 2-3 minutes each. The rest of the group listens and notes patterns on stickies.

**Key prompts:**
- How did they find you?
- What was the problem they were trying to solve?
- What made the deal happen (or what made the relationship difficult)?
- Would you want 10 more like them? Why or why not?

**What it produces:** A set of real customer stories that ground the rest of the workshop in evidence, not assumptions. The patterns across stories become the raw material for ICP definition.

### Exercise 2: Pattern Wall (20 min)

Take the stickies from Exercise 1 and cluster them into categories: company characteristics, persona traits, pain points, buying triggers, red flags.

**Key prompts:**
- What do the "best" stories have in common?
- What do the "worst" stories have in common?
- Are there traits that predict success or failure before the deal closes?
- Is there a difference between who buys and who uses?

**What it produces:** A visual map of customer patterns organized by category. The clusters reveal the natural ICP segments.

### Exercise 3: Segment Prioritization (15 min)

From the pattern clusters, name 2-3 distinct customer segments. For each: who they are, what they need, and why they're a good fit.

**Key prompts:**
- If you could only sell to one of these segments for the next 12 months, which one?
- Which segment has the shortest sales cycle?
- Which segment has the highest lifetime value?
- Which segment do you understand best?

**What it produces:** A ranked list of ICP segments with a clear primary segment and rationale for the ranking.

### Exercise 4: Qualifying Criteria (15 min)

For the primary ICP segment, define the specific criteria that identify a good-fit prospect and the red flags that disqualify.

**Key prompts:**
- What 3-5 things must be true for a prospect to be a great fit?
- What signals should make you walk away?
- At what moment in the customer's lifecycle are they most likely to need you?

**What it produces:** A qualifying framework the sales team (or founder) can apply to every new prospect.

## Expected outputs

1. **ICP definition document** with 2-3 segments, each described by: company profile, buyer persona, pain points, buying triggers, and qualifying criteria
2. **Primary ICP segment** clearly identified with rationale
3. **Qualifying/disqualifying criteria** for the primary segment
4. **Customer language bank** of actual phrases and descriptions from the stories exercise (useful for positioning and content)

## What typically comes next

- **Positioning Workshop** to craft messaging that speaks directly to the primary ICP in their language
- **Growth Channel Workshop** to identify the best channels for reaching the defined ICP
- Update the company profile with the new ICP definition
- Review current marketing and sales materials against the new ICP criteria

## Board layout

**Frame 1: Customer Stories**
Two columns: "Best Customers" and "Worst Customers." Each participant gets a row. Sticky note prompts in each cell: "How they found us," "The problem they had," "What made it work / not work."

**Frame 2: Pattern Wall**
Five columns: "Company Traits," "Persona Traits," "Pain Points," "Buying Triggers," "Red Flags." Participants move and cluster stickies from Frame 1 into these categories.

**Frame 3: Segments**
Three boxes (one per segment). Each box has sections: "Who they are," "What they need," "Why they're a good fit," "Priority rank."

**Frame 4: Qualifying Criteria**
Two columns: "Must be true (qualify)" and "Walk away (disqualify)." Primary segment name at the top. 3-5 stickies per column.

**Frame 5: Outputs and Next Steps**
Summary section with the primary ICP statement, key qualifying criteria, and assigned next steps with owners and dates.
