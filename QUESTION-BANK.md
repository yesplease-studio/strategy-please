# Question Bank

Questions organized by strategic topic. Not a script — a bank. Select based on what the situation calls for. Over time, note which questions produce the most useful insights and in which contexts.

Each question includes guidance on what the answer reveals and what to listen for.

---

## Purpose, Mission, and Vision

The "Why → How → What" questions. Most companies can describe What they do. Fewer can articulate How they do it distinctively. Very few can state Why in a way that's authentic and useful for positioning.

### Why questions (the belief)

- **"Why did you start this company?"** — The origin story. Listen for whether the answer is a personal frustration that drove action or a market opportunity that was identified analytically. Both are valid, but personal frustration tends to produce more authentic positioning.

- **"If your company disappeared tomorrow, what would be lost?"** — Forces them to articulate the unique value they bring to the world. If the answer is "our customers would find an alternative easily," that's a positioning problem. If the answer is specific and emotional, that's the Why.

- **"What do you believe about your industry that most people get wrong?"** — Surfaces the contrarian bet underneath the company. Great positioning is often built on a belief that the market hasn't fully accepted yet. If they can't name one, the company may be a "better mousetrap" rather than a genuinely different approach.

- **"What would you keep doing even if the company failed?"** — Separates the mission from the vehicle. The answer reveals what's genuinely important to the founder vs. what's commercially convenient.

### How questions (the approach)

- **"What principles guide how you build your product / deliver your service?"** — The operating beliefs. "We believe in transparency" is generic. "We show customers exactly how the algorithm works because trust is more important than protecting IP" is a real principle that affects product and positioning.

- **"What do you refuse to do that competitors are willing to do?"** — The boundaries reveal the values. Saying no to something commercially viable because it conflicts with a principle is a powerful positioning signal.

- **"How do you make decisions when there's no clear data?"** — Reveals the company's intuition and culture. Some companies are data-driven to a fault (analysis paralysis). Others move fast on instinct. Neither is inherently better, but understanding which mode they operate in affects every recommendation.

### What questions (the offering)

- **"Explain what you do in one sentence — as if you're telling a friend at a dinner party."** — The simplicity test. If the answer takes 30 seconds, the positioning isn't clear enough. The variance between how different stakeholders answer this question is itself diagnostic.

- **"What do your happiest customers say about you that you didn't expect?"** — Surfaces the real value proposition, which is often different from the marketed one. The words customers use unprompted are the most powerful positioning inputs.

## Revenue Model and Pricing

Understanding how a company makes money reveals more about its strategy than its mission statement does. Pricing decisions expose what the company values, who it builds for, and where it sees its competitive advantage.

- **"How do you make money?"** — Start here. The answer should be simple. If it takes more than two sentences, the model may be too complex or not well understood internally.

- **"What's your pricing model, and why that model?"** — The "why" is more important than the numbers. Usage-based pricing says something different about the company than seat-based pricing. The reasoning reveals strategic thinking (or lack thereof).

- **"What does your average customer pay, and what does your best customer pay?"** — The ratio between these two numbers reveals whether the company has figured out expansion revenue. A 10x difference suggests a healthy land-and-expand motion. A 2x difference suggests flat pricing with little upsell.

- **"Who are you too expensive for, and who are you too cheap for?"** — Forces them to think about pricing as positioning. Being too cheap for enterprise signals something about the product and brand. Being too expensive for startups signals something else. Both are valid — but the company should know which trade-off it's making.

- **"What would happen if you doubled your prices tomorrow?"** — Reveals pricing confidence. If the answer is "we'd lose everyone," the product may be commoditized or the ICP may be wrong. If the answer is "our best customers wouldn't blink," there's pricing power being left on the table.

- **"What percentage of revenue comes from your top 10% of customers?"** — Revenue concentration risk. High concentration means a few churned accounts could be existential. It also means the company's real ICP might be those top customers, not the average ones.

## Customer and ICP

Questions that go deeper than "who do you sell to" into the actual behavior, pain, and decision-making of the customer.

- **"Tell me about the last customer you closed. Walk me through how they found you, what the conversation was like, and what made them buy."** — A single detailed story reveals more than a persona document. Listen for specifics: what channel, what pain, what objection, what tipped the decision.

- **"Who are your best customers, and what makes them 'best'?"** — Defines "best" — is it revenue, retention, advocacy, ease of working with? The answer reveals what the company optimizes for.

- **"Who have you lost recently, and why?"** — Churn stories are often more diagnostic than win stories. Listen for patterns: same competitor? Same objection? Same company profile?

- **"If you could clone one customer 100 times, who would it be and why?"** — Forces ICP prioritization. The answer should be specific enough to build a targeting list from.

- **"What does your customer's day look like before they use your product?"** — Surfaces the daily friction, not the annual pain. Products that solve daily friction get used. Products that solve annual pain get evaluated once and forgotten.

- **"How do your customers describe their problem — in their own words, not yours?"** — If the company can answer this with actual quotes, they're close to their customers. If they answer with internal product language, there's a gap.

- **"Are you selling to the person who feels the pain, the person who holds the budget, or both? And are they the same person?"** — The buyer vs. user distinction is critical. Different people need different messages.

## Competitive Landscape

Questions about how the company sees its competitive position and whether that view is accurate.

- **"Who do you lose deals to most often?"** — More useful than "who are your competitors." The answer reveals the actual competitive set, which may be different from the perceived one.

- **"When a prospect is comparing you to [competitor], what do they usually ask?"** — Surfaces the real evaluation criteria, not the marketing comparison points.

- **"What does [competitor] do better than you?"** — Tests honesty and self-awareness. A company that can't admit competitive weaknesses will produce inauthentic positioning.

- **"When you win, why do you win? When you lose, why do you lose?"** — The win/loss pattern reveals the actual differentiation — not the claimed one.

- **"Who is your most dangerous competitor, and why?"** — Distinguishes between the biggest competitor (market share) and the most dangerous one (trajectory, approach, positioning).

- **"Is your biggest competitor another product, the status quo (doing nothing), or an internal workaround (spreadsheets, manual processes)?"** — Many companies are really competing against inertia, not against other products. The sales approach changes dramatically depending on the answer.

## Growth and Go-to-Market

Questions about how the company acquires, converts, and retains customers.

- **"How do customers find you today?"** — Start with reality, not aspiration. The answer reveals the current growth engine (or lack thereof). Listen for whether they know the answer with data or are guessing.

- **"What's the single most effective thing you've done to get customers?"** — Forces prioritization. If the answer is "referrals" or "word of mouth," that's valuable — but it may not scale. If the answer is a specific campaign or channel, ask what made it work.

- **"What marketing or sales activity have you tried that didn't work?"** — The failure history. Listen for what they attribute the failure to. External factors ("the market wasn't ready") vs. internal factors ("we didn't execute well enough") vs. strategic factors ("we were targeting the wrong people") are very different diagnoses.

- **"If you had to get 10 new customers in 90 days and you could only do one thing, what would it be?"** — Forces constraint-based thinking. The answer reveals what they believe is highest-leverage. If they can't answer, the growth strategy is unclear.

- **"What's your sales cycle look like? How long from first touch to closed deal?"** — The length and complexity of the sales cycle determines the entire GTM approach. A 2-week cycle needs different content, different outbound, and different follow-up than a 6-month cycle.

- **"Where do deals stall, and what happens when they stall?"** — Reveals the friction points in the sales process. Common answers: "after the demo," "when they need to get budget approval," "when they go to evaluate competitors." Each has a different fix.

- **"Are you investing in activities that get you in front of the right people — not just more people?"** — The efficiency question. A brand awareness campaign that reaches 10,000 people who aren't in the ICP is less valuable than a targeted effort that reaches 100 who are. This is especially relevant when the "what keeps you up at night" answer is about money/runway — every marketing dollar needs to connect to revenue.

## Internal Alignment and Team

Questions about whether the organization is aligned on strategy and has the capacity to execute.

- **"If I asked your three most senior people to describe what your company does, would I get the same answer?"** — Internal alignment test. Misalignment at the top cascades into confused messaging, inconsistent sales conversations, and scattered product decisions.

- **"Who owns marketing? Who owns sales? Are those the right people?"** — Reveals organizational capacity and gaps. "The founder does everything" is a different situation than "we have a marketing lead but they're stretched thin."

- **"What's the one thing your team spends time on that you wish they didn't have to?"** — Surfaces operational drag that's consuming capacity that should go toward growth.

- **"If you hired one person tomorrow, what role would it be and why?"** — Reveals the most acute capability gap as the company perceives it.

- **"How do you decide what to work on next?"** — Reveals the planning and prioritization process (or lack thereof). Ad hoc decision-making vs. structured planning tells you a lot about organizational maturity.

## Strategic Positioning and Messaging

Questions specifically about how the company communicates its value — the bridge between strategy and execution.

- **"What's the one thing you want every prospect to believe after talking to you?"** — The core message, stripped of complexity. If they can state it clearly, positioning is probably in decent shape. If they list five things, it's not.

- **"If you had to put your entire pitch on a billboard, what would it say?"** — The simplicity constraint forces clarity. Billboards don't have room for "we're an AI-powered platform that leverages..."

- **"What do your customers tell their friends about you?"** — The referral pitch — the most honest version of the positioning because it's in the customer's words, not the company's.

- **"Open your website right now. Does the homepage explain what you do, for whom, and why it matters — in the first scroll?"** — Real-time positioning audit. Most companies' websites describe features, not outcomes. This question makes the gap visible immediately.

- **"What's the story? How did this product come to exist, and why now?"** — Narrative positioning. The founding story, if authentic, is one of the most powerful positioning tools available. But it needs to be told well and connected to the customer's situation.

## Financial Health and Runway

Questions that surface the financial context for strategic decisions. Not about getting exact numbers — about understanding the constraints.

- **"Are you default alive?"** — Paul Graham's framing. If the company's revenue growth trend continues and expenses stay flat, will they reach profitability before running out of money? If not, every strategic decision needs to be evaluated against the runway clock.

- **"How long is your runway at current burn?"** — Context for urgency. A company with 6 months of runway needs different advice than one with 24 months. The strategy recommendations must respect this reality.

- **"Are you looking for investors, customers, or both?"** — When "money" is what keeps them up at night, this question determines the entire priority structure. Investor-seeking companies need a different narrative and set of metrics than customer-seeking companies. Though often: the best way to attract investors is to get customers.

- **"What would getting to $X revenue unlock for you?"** — Reveals the milestones that matter and what they'd make possible (hiring, fundraising, profitability). The number itself matters less than what it represents.

## Usage notes

**This is a question bank, not a script.** Select questions based on what the situation calls for. Using all of them with one company would take days. The skill is knowing which 5-10 questions are most diagnostic for this specific situation.

**Questions improve through use.** After each engagement, note which questions produced the most useful insights and which fell flat. Over time, the bank will develop annotations about context-effectiveness:

```
<!-- effectiveness: high for pre-PMF companies, low for scale-ups -->
<!-- pairs well with: "who have you lost recently" -->
<!-- often reveals: misalignment between founder vision and market reality -->
```

**Questions are conversation starters, not checkboxes.** The best diagnostic information often comes from the follow-up: "Tell me more about that," "What happened next?", "What did you learn from that?" The bank provides the opening; the conversation provides the insight.
